Sales Play

Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager

For BDRs, AEs, SEs, CSMs, PS, and Partners. This play sells Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager into privacy and legal teams who are drowning in data subject requests. Pick your motion above, then move through the play.

Core positioning: Other privacy platforms automate intake and discovery. Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager governs the entire data subject rights lifecycle, from multi-brand intake through defensible review, redaction, production, deletion, and audit, in one platform. Its sharpest edge is the hard cases (complex and employee DSRs) where review, privilege, and defensibility matter most.
The play (Expansion): You are selling into an account that already trusts Exterro for e-discovery or legal hold. Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager is the privacy extension of a platform they already run. Lead with that. They reuse the review and collection muscle they already have, consolidate a vendor, and close the gap their privacy point tool leaves open.
Opening line: "Since your team already runs Exterro for [e-discovery / legal hold], I wanted to flag that Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager extends that same platform to privacy requests, reusing your review and collection muscle for access, deletion, and employee DSRs. Worth a short conversation?"
The play (New Logo): Net-new account, displacing a manual process (email, spreadsheets, PDF redaction) or a privacy point tool. Do not lead with category education. Lead with the wedge: integrated review and redaction, employee-DSR strength, and defensibility. That is where the incumbent and the point tools break down.
Opening line: "A lot of privacy and legal teams tell me DSR fulfillment has become a manual scramble: data scattered across systems, redaction in PDFs, and no clean audit trail for regulators. How is your team handling that today?"

Triggers & Scenarios

When to Run This Play

Four scenarios that signal a fit. Match what you are hearing to one of these, then use the open it gives you. The strongest is the first one: the hard cases are where the wedge is sharpest.

1. Hard or employee DSRs that mirror e-discovery Lead Trigger

What you'll hear

"Employee access requests touch HR, email, Slack, SharePoint, and Drive." "We are worried about producing privileged or co-mingled third-party data." "Reviewing it all by hand takes forever."

Why it's a fit

Consumer-grade privacy tools were not built for custodian-based collection or privilege review. This is the wedge: native e-discovery-grade review and redaction inside the platform.

How to open

"How do you handle employee DSRs today, the ones that involve privileged or third-party data across HR and email?"

Where it goes

Push to a review and redaction demo and an employee-DSR workflow walkthrough.

2. High cost per request (outsourcing the hard ones)

What you'll hear

"We send the complex requests to outside counsel or a vendor." "Each one costs us a small fortune." Low annual volume but high cost per request.

Why it's a fit

The buy is driven by cost per request, not volume. One Exterro customer bought at only 5 to 6 DSARs a year because a single outsourced request ran roughly $20K. Internal / anecdotal

How to open

"When a complex request comes in, who handles it, and what does that one request cost you by the time it is done?"

Where it goes

Frame the platform against the per-request outsourcing spend. Do not present the $20K figure as a benchmark; use it to make the cost real.

3. Rising volume or missed deadlines

What you'll hear

"Requests keep climbing." "We set up backend records by hand for every one." "Tasks live in different systems and deadlines slip." Multiple intake mailboxes and forms per brand.

Why it's a fit

GDPR runs a 30-day clock and CCPA a 45-day clock. Manual NCDS setup and fragmented intake do not scale. Automated record creation, task orchestration, and SLA tracking fix the slippage.

How to open

"Have you missed a regulatory response deadline in the last year? Where does time actually go on a request?"

Where it goes

Show unified intake, auto record creation, and the centralized SLA dashboard.

4. Expanding multi-state or multi-jurisdiction exposure

What you'll hear

"New state laws keep coming." "We just expanded into the EU." "Different brands and regions, one small team." A new privacy leader or a recent breach often surfaces this.

Why it's a fit

Requests carry deadlines across GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, and a patchwork of 19+ US state laws. One console runs separate branded portals, forms, and workflows across jurisdictions.

How to open

"Do you operate multiple brands or jurisdictions on one team? How do you keep them straight today?"

Where it goes

Demo multi-brand configuration: each brand gets its own portal, form, and workflow, centrally administered.

Sensitive and privileged data is protected before release, with less manual effort and full defensibility. This is what most DSAR platforms hand off elsewhere.

Objection Handling

Handle the Pushback

Tap to expand. The first objection changes with your motion. The rest apply to both. Tags show where each one fits.

New Logo"We already have OneTrust / a privacy tool."▾
OneTrust is a compliance-workflow layer. It exports documents to external tools for review and creates backend records by hand. Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager keeps review, redaction, and chain of custody native in Vault and auto-creates the records. That is the defensibility and labor savings a compliance-workflow tool cannot match. Do not claim they cannot redact, they can. Differentiate on the integrated review environment and the connected lifecycle.
Expansion"Is this a need-to-have right now?"▾
One platform that lowers risk and cost and consolidates vendors. You already trust Exterro for e-discovery or legal hold, so this is the privacy extension of a platform you run, not a new bet. Tie it to active exposure (a missed deadline, new state-law coverage, employee-DSR risk) and to what the complex requests cost you when they go outside.
Expansion"We don't have bandwidth to learn another legal tool."▾
It is the same platform your team already uses. Tasks arrive with clear scope, uploads happen inside the task, and there is no separate instance to stand up or learn. The lift is low because the muscle is already there.
Both"Isn't native redaction just redaction? Other vendors do that too."▾
Correct, redaction alone is not unique. OneTrust, Transcend, and BigID redact, and EU review and redaction specialists like Nuix and Phaselaw compete here too. Do not claim sole ownership. Shift the wedge to the full governed lifecycle (intake, identity verification, automated record creation, collection, production, deletion, audit) and to e-discovery-grade, custodian-based collection for complex and employee DSRs. That is the part competitors do not run end to end.
Both"We are mostly consumer DSRs. Why do we need e-discovery-grade review?"▾
Even consumer requests carry third-party and co-mingled data, so over-disclosure is a live risk. The wedge is the hard ones, and having that capability protects you on every request. If employee DSRs are anywhere in scope, the case is stronger still.
Both"Each of our brands is different."▾
Each brand gets its own portal, form, and workflow, centrally administered from one console. No separate instances to run. That is multi-brand by design, not a workaround.
Both"Vendor X has thousands more connectors."▾
Do not compete on raw connector count, that is not where we win. Position on persistent data-inventory linkage and review depth. Discovery at request time misses legacy, unconnected, and M&A systems, and a long connector list does not give you native review, redaction, or defensibility.

Messaging Guardrails

Stay Defensible

What to say, what to avoid, and the naming rules. Claim only what we can stand behind, and refresh competitive intel per deal.

Do

  • Lead with governed lifecycle, defensibility, employee-DSR strength, and platform consolidation
  • Say "automates intake, record creation, and orchestration" and "rule-based auto-redaction with human review"
  • Position review and redaction as native and integrated in Vault
  • Refresh competitor capabilities and certifications live, per deal

Don't

  • Do not call it "agentic AI" or say "the AI decides what to release." A human reviews before any release
  • Do not claim sole ownership of automated redaction
  • Do not compete on raw connector count
  • Do not claim a competitor "cannot redact." Differentiate on the lifecycle
  • Do not quote pricing

Naming & Positioning Rules

Rule 1: The full product name is Exterro Data Subject Rights Manager; use it in customer-facing materials and always lead with Exterro. "DSR Manager" and "DSRM" are internal shorthand only. "DSAR" is a request type, not the product name.
Rule 2: On Nuix and Phaselaw in the EU, do not position Exterro as the only platform that can review or redact. Differentiate on the governed end-to-end lifecycle and defensible, custodian-based collection.
Safe line to reuse: "Other privacy platforms automate intake and discovery. Exterro governs the whole lifecycle, from intake through defensible review, redaction, production, and audit, in one platform."
